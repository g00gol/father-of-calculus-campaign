Sir Isaac Newton

by Madi Crum

January 4, 1643 - March 31, 1727

Isaac Newton's Early Life

Born in 1643 in Woolsthorpe, England was Isaac Newton. He was a son to an uneducated but wealthy farmer, who died some time before Newton was born. This caused his mother to remarry, and Newton was left with his grandmother. Although Newton was not the smartest at his school, he was offered to attend Trinity College Cambridge. He wanted to study law, but his mother would not pay for his education, leaving him to work as a servant during his time at college.

Meanwhile, Newton became incredibly curious about mathematics after buying a math book and not understanding the information it contained. He earned a bachelor’s degree in 1665 but was prevented from further education due to the plague. His college was closed due to the highly contagious disease (as I am sure we can all relate to), and Newton went home. He took this time in quarantine to pursue his own ideas in mathematics, physics, optics, and astronomy. When the college reopened, he took a fellowship to earn his master’s degree.

He had finished working on his three laws of motion by 1666, and later his works on universal gravitation, diffraction of light, centripetal force, and many other variations of studies. He became a leader in scientific research after all his remarkable works. However, his work came to a halt when he suffered a nervous breakdown in 1679.

After regaining his health, he once again continued to study and spoke out against King James II when he only wanted Roman Catholics to be in positions of government and academia. Once William of Orange chased King James II out of England, Newton was elected to Parliament, leading him to become more interested with the life of politics than research.

In 1693, Newton suffered from another nervous breakdown and ultimately retired from research. Newton was a man who was torn between his want for fame and fear of criticism, and he resisted publishing his work immediately because of this. He often felt like he had to defend his work against plagiarism, one dispute like this being between him and Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz about calculus. This dispute dominated the last years of Newton's life. Newton died in 1727.

Isaac’s Incredible Innovations

During the Great Plague of 1665-1666, Newton accomplished many things: he discovered a new theory of light, uncovered and quantified gravitation, and forged a world-changing approach to mathematics: infinitesimal calculus. Newton’s invention of calculus was the most important link between his laws of motion and universal gravitation. In fact, his focus on gravity and laws of motion brought him to his breakthrough in calculus. He once tried to describe the speed of a falling object; however, rate of change and the issue of movement had not been investigated yet, so this led him to try and fill this void of mathematics.

Newton began incorporating planetary ellipses into his initial theory to try and explain the orbits of the planets. By using calculus, Newton explained the movement of planets and why the orbits were in the shape of an ellipse. This led him to his discovery explaining that the gravitational force that keeps us on the ground is the same force that causes the planets to orbit the sun and the moon to orbit the Earth.

When Newton developed the idea of calculus and its focus on the rate of change, it helped tremendously with his previous work of physics, and he found that both mathematics and physics are ultimately linked together through calculus. Calculus was, and still is, very important because it allowed mathematicians and engineers to understand the motion and dynamic change in the ever-changing world around us, like planetary orbits and fluids.

Major Contributions:

The Average Slope of a Curve - Newton’s initial problem was that the slope of a curve was always varying, and there was no method to pinpoint the exact slope at any one individual point on a curve, like the slope of a tangent line to the curve at that point. Newton was able to calculate a derivative function which gave the slope at any point of a function, which is part of differential calculus or differentiation (which Newton called the “method of fluxions”). In a case considering time and distance, the slope would represent the speed of the object at a certain point.

Method of Fluents - This was the “opposite” of differentiation and was part of integral calculus or integration. Integration and differentiation are the two main operations of calculus. Differentiation and integration are inverse operations, so that, if a function is first integrated and then differentiated (or vice versa), the original function is retrieved, as stated by Newton’s Fundamental Theorem of Calculus.

Is Isaac the "Father of Calculus"?

The controversy remains that Isaac Newton was the first to discover and use calculus, but Wilhelm Leibniz was the first to publish his works and spread it across Europe. The most modern version of calculus was drawn out by Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz. However, my argument can be compared to the likes of psychology: Wilhelm Wundt was deemed the father of psychology, yet numerous people have come up with their own ideas afterwards to better improve how we study psychology. The same kind of logic can be applied to calculus. Newton went through two nervous breakdowns during his life and was torn between his desire for fame and his fear for criticism, which is why he hesitated on publishing his work. This can be understandable, especially in a world full of many things to discover and put your name on. I believe that Newton should be deemed the father of calculus because he came up with many of the original concepts needed for calculus.

One theory and discovery, like Newton’s calculus, can act as a starting point for others that come after it. Without the use of calculus, Albert Einstein would not have been able to derive his astonishing theory of relativity. Nowadays, calculus is used in many fields, including math, science, engineering, biology, and more. Social sciences must rely on calculus heavily to calculate birth rates and death rates. The same can be said about economic forecasts and many other fields, like engineering and medical industries. Personally, I believe that Isaac Newton and his invention of calculus has had an incredible impact on the world around us and how we see it, or measure it, if you will.
