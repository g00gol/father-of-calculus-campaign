Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz

by Jackey Yang

July 1, 1646 - November 14, 1716

Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz's Early Life

Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz was a German mathematician renown as a prodigy for his works in philosophy, engineering, physics, law, and politics, among other things. He was born in Saxony, Germany on July 1, 1646. His father and mother were both prominent figures in the city as the former was a professor of moral philosophy and the latter being the daughter of a successful local lawyer. Unfortunately, when Leibniz was only 6, his father died.

Despite this, Leibniz was considered a prodigy in his childhood, studying Greek works and Latin at the young age of 12. At the age of 14, he studied philosophy, math, and law at the University of Leipzig; when he applied for a doctorate, he was denied due to his age. However, after Leibniz presented an incredible thesis to the University of Altdorf, the professors promptly awarded him a doctorate and professorship.

Leibniz’s life speaks in ways not many can, and his contributions lay the foundation for modern mathematics – or rather, the world as a whole – in which we carry on today. At the age of 70, Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz died in Hanover on November 14, 1716.

Wilhelm's Wondrous Works

It is not lightly put when I describe Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz's works, having laid the foundation to our understanding of contemporary calculus. Though, Leibniz primarily contributed work to philosophy and logic while working as a politician in the great majority of his day. Despite this, somehow, he continually spared hours to study and work on mathematics and later developed a system of infinitesimal calculus. Leibniz also restored the ancient method of solving equations through matrices and developed the use of binary. independently from his "rival" Sir Isaac Newton. I find it important to note that both Leibniz and Newton held off on publishing their works, but Leibniz had published his ideas in 1684 while Newton's works were published in the 18th century.

By many accounts, Leibniz is said to have known and be fully aware of Newton's works in calculus. However, it is unstated that either men plagiarized the other's work; furthermore, each presents specifics of calculus that are particularly theirs. In Leibniz's case, he used and created the contemporary notation of calculus. For this fact, he had much improved upon the clumsy and lacking notation Newton had used. Along with this, Leibniz also re-introduced a form of arranging linear equations we know as a matrix, allowing easier manipulation and solution. In effect, over 300 years later, our contemporary and modern form of calculus most directly compares to that of Leibniz.

Is Wilhelm the "Father of Calculus"?

For what it is worth, both men sought to create their respective versions of calculus and succeeded, and they have shaped the world today from three millenniums ago. However, I believe that Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz deserves the recognition as the "Father of Calculus" as he has subsequently provided the most concurrent form of calculus while providing the clearest. While Newton may have, on record, conceived of the idea of calculus before him, Leibniz was still the ultimate progenitor of modern and complete calculus: what Newton writes seems to be incomplete, while Leibniz's was complete and coherent.

Furthermore, I must speak of this debate in another way. As Leibniz published his works, he received attacks from Newton that accused him of plagiarism. In extension, Newton was the president of the Royal Society in London that was asked to arbitrate between the claims. The ruling, logically biased, gave credit to Newton for calculus's conception and credit of publication to Wilhelm. However, the aforementioned conception of calculus was brittle and unpolished unlike the publication of Wilhelm's work.

In an extended analogy, Newton's findings may have been the foundation for the modern airplane but Leibniz was the one to piece and build the structure. For this, Newton simply conceptualized an idea that had no start or end while Leibniz built a reality from the theories Newton had began.
